Acid-Base pH + pOH = 14

1. pH & pOH Relation

💡 Key Takeaway

Linear inverse relationship at 25°C (298 K). As pH increases, pOH decreases linearly with a constant slope of -1.

Acid-Base pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]

2. pH & [H⁺] Relation

💡 Key Takeaway

Logarithmic decay curve. As hydrogen ion concentration [H⁺] increases toward 1.0 M, pH drops sharply toward 0.

Acid-Base pOH = 14 + log₁₀[H⁺]

3. pOH & [H⁺] Relation

💡 Key Takeaway

Logarithmic growth curve. As [H⁺] increases, [OH⁻] decreases proportionally, causing pOH to rise logarithmically toward 14.

Acid-Base pOH = -log₁₀[OH⁻]

4. pOH & [OH⁻] Relation

💡 Key Takeaway

Identical logarithmic decay behavior as pH vs [H⁺]. Higher hydroxide ion concentration yields a lower pOH value.

Thermodynamics ln(K) ∝ -ΔH° / RT

5. Kc vs Temperature (Exo vs Endo)

💡 Key Takeaway

Endothermic reactions (ΔH > 0) gain product yield as T rises (Kc increases). Exothermic reactions (ΔH < 0) lose yield.

Thermodynamics K = Constant at fixed T

6. Kc / Kp vs Concentration

💡 Key Takeaway

Classic Exam Trap! Equilibrium constants (Kc or Kp) are strictly independent of concentration, volume, or pressure changes.

Reaction Progress Reactants → Products

7. Conc. vs Time (Complete Rxn)

💡 Key Takeaway

Irreversible reaction goes to completion. Reactant concentration drops to zero, while product yield reaches theoretical maximum.

Reaction Progress Kc = [Prod] / [React] = 1

8. Reversible Rxn (Kc = 1)

💡 Key Takeaway

At equilibrium time teq, reactant and product concentrations become strictly equal ([Prod] = [React]) and remain constant.

Reaction Progress Kc = [Prod] / [React] > 1

9. Reversible Rxn (Kc > 1)

💡 Key Takeaway

Products favored! At equilibrium, product concentration exceeds reactant concentration. Product curve crosses above reactant curve.

Reaction Progress Kc = [Prod] / [React] < 1

10. Reversible Rxn (Kc < 1)

💡 Key Takeaway

Reactants favored! Equilibrium is reached before much product is formed. Reactant concentration remains higher than products.

Reaction Speed Rate = k · [C]¹

11. Rate vs Concentration

💡 Key Takeaway

Higher reactant concentration increases effective collision frequency. For 1st order kinetics, speed is directly linear with [C].

Reaction Speed k = A · e^(-Ea / RT)

12. Rate vs Temperature

💡 Key Takeaway

Arrhenius Equation. Speed increases exponentially with temperature because significantly more molecules exceed activation energy Ea.

Reaction Speed Rate ∝ Exposed Area

13. Rate vs Surface Area

💡 Key Takeaway

Subdividing solids (powdering) multiplies exposed surface area, increasing contact collision sites and accelerating reaction rate.

Reaction Speed r(forward) = r(reverse)

14. Fwd & Rev Rates vs Time

💡 Key Takeaway

Forward speed r1 starts high and drops. Reverse speed r2 starts at 0 and grows. At equilibrium time teq, both speeds become equal.

Ionization α = √(Ka · V)

15. Ionization (α) vs Dilution

💡 Key Takeaway

Ostwald's Dilution Law. As volume V increases (dilution), weak electrolyte ionization α increases asymptotically toward 1 (100%).

Ionization α = √(Ka / C)

16. Ionization (α) vs Conc.

💡 Key Takeaway

Inverse square root relationship. As solute concentration C increases, degree of ionization decreases due to ion association.

Ionization Δng = n(prod) - n(react)

17. Pressure & Moles Shift

💡 Key Takeaway

Le Chatelier's Principle. Higher pressure favors the side with fewer gas moles. If Δng = 0, pressure has zero effect on yield.
